JPG vs PNG — What's the difference?

Before converting, it helps to understand what makes these formats different and when to use each one.

When should you convert JPG to PNG?

There are several common situations where converting a JPG to PNG makes sense:

  • You need transparency. PNG supports transparent backgrounds, JPG does not. If you need to remove a background or place an image on a colored background, PNG is essential.
  • You're editing the image repeatedly. Every time you save a JPG it re-compresses and loses quality. PNG is lossless so it stays perfect no matter how many times you save it.
  • You need pixel-perfect quality. For screenshots, UI mockups, diagrams, and graphics, PNG preserves every detail.
  • The image has text or sharp edges. JPG can make text and sharp lines look blurry. PNG keeps them crisp.
💡 Quick tip

Converting a JPG to PNG won't recover quality that was already lost during JPG compression. PNG is lossless going forward, but can't undo previous compression. For best results, start with the highest quality JPG you have.

Will the quality change when converting JPG to PNG?

The short answer is no — converting JPG to PNG will not reduce image quality further. PNG is a lossless format, meaning it preserves every pixel exactly as-is. However, it's important to understand that any quality loss that occurred when the image was originally saved as a JPG cannot be recovered. Converting to PNG simply stops any further quality loss from occurring.

Why is my PNG file larger than the JPG?

This is completely normal. PNG files are typically 2-5x larger than equivalent JPG files because PNG uses lossless compression — it preserves every pixel perfectly. JPG achieves smaller file sizes by permanently discarding some image data. If file size is a priority and you don't need transparency, stick with JPG. If quality and transparency matter more, PNG is the right choice.
